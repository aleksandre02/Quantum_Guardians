WHAT I IMPLEMENTED — FOR SUBMISSION
================================================================

Project: Education Realm — Card-Based Tactical RPG
Engine:  Godot 4.2 | Language: GDScript

----------------------------------------------------------------

OVERVIEW

Education Realm is a turn-based card game built in Godot 4.2.
The theme is education and social equity — the three card types
(Access, Quality, and Equity) represent the core pillars of a
fair education system. The player fights an enemy that embodies
systemic barriers, and must strategically play cards each turn
to weaken it across all three dimensions before pressure
overwhelms them.

The most distinctive design choice in this game is that its
core mechanics are directly inspired by quantum mechanics —
specifically superposition and decoherence. These are not just
cosmetic labels; the actual gameplay logic models these concepts
in a meaningful way, as described below.

----------------------------------------------------------------

QUANTUM MECHANICS IN THE GAME

The central question this game asks is: what does it mean to
"fight" an abstract systemic problem like unequal access to
education? The answer I explored was to make the enemy's
weakness genuinely uncertain — not just hidden, but
mathematically undefined until observed, just like a quantum
system.

SUPERPOSITION
Each of the three stats (Access, Quality, Equity) exists in a
superposition of possible values rather than a single known
number. When the player plays a card, they do not directly
increase a stat — instead they widen the range of what that
stat could become. This is represented visually with three
layered bars per stat:

  - The coloured bar shows the last confirmed (observed) value.
  - The white bar shows the upper bound of the possible range.
  - The dark bar shows the lower bound of the possible range.

The gap between the dark and white bars is the superposition —
the space of all outcomes that are currently possible. The
player is essentially influencing probability, not certainty.
The range expands asymmetrically when a card is played
(low end gains 60% of the card's power, high end gains 140%)
to create genuine tension around whether the collapse will
land well or poorly.

WAVE FUNCTION COLLAPSE
When the player presses End Turn, the superposition collapses.
Each stat resolves to a random value uniformly drawn from its
current [min, max] range. This is the wave function collapse —
the act of observation forces the system into a definite state.
The player cannot know in advance where the value will land,
only that playing more cards of that type makes a better
outcome more likely by shifting the range upward.

DECOHERENCE
Decoherence in quantum physics refers to a quantum system
losing its superposition prematurely due to unwanted
interaction with its environment. In this game, decoherence
is a risk mechanic that triggers randomly as the player plays
more cards. Each card played doubles the probability of a
decoherence event occurring. When it fires:

  - One random stat's superposition collapses back to its
	last confirmed value, erasing all the progress the player
	built up on that stat this turn.
  - The enemy retaliates — an attack animation plays, the
	screen shakes, and the player takes a symbolic hit.
  - The decoherence bar resets and a message identifies which
	stat collapsed.
  - If decoherence happens 5 times across the whole game,
	the system is considered fully destabilised and the
	player loses.

This mechanic creates a genuine dilemma: playing more cards
gives a better range but raises the risk of decoherence
undoing it all. The player must decide when enough superposition
has been built up and it is time to collapse it safely via
End Turn.

The decoherence probability formula is: chance = numerator / 526,
where numerator starts at 1 and doubles after each safe card.
This means the first card has a ~0.19% chance, the second ~0.38%,
and so on — it escalates quickly if the player is greedy.

----------------------------------------------------------------

PRESSURE MECHANIC

To prevent the player from stalling indefinitely, a pressure
system escalates over time. Each End Turn increases the
pressure bar by a rising amount following the schedule
[5, 8, 12, 17, 23, 30, 38, 47]. Reaching 100 pressure is a
loss condition. A ghost preview bar always shows how much
pressure the next End Turn will cost, giving the player
information to weigh risk vs. reward each turn.

----------------------------------------------------------------

CARD SYSTEM

The player holds 4 cards at a time, each randomly typed as
Access, Quality, or Equity. Playing a card sends it to the
deck with a smooth animation and deals a fresh random card
back into the hand. At End Turn, all four cards are reshuffled
with staggered fly-to-deck animations. Cards are locked during
animations to prevent unintended double inputs.

Cards have diminishing returns within a single turn — playing
the same card type repeatedly is less efficient (100%, 80%,
60%, minimum 20%), encouraging variety and pushing the player
to spread their effort across all three stats.

----------------------------------------------------------------

VISUAL FEEDBACK & POLISH

A card hover effect scales cards up slightly when the mouse
moves over them, with a springy overshoot animation, giving
the hand a tactile feel. The stat labels (A, Q, E) change
colour from their base colour to yellow after End Turn when
their stat has been fully maxed, but only after the collapse —
not during card play — so the player does not know for certain
whether they succeeded until they commit.

Screen shake is implemented on decoherence events to
emphasise the disruption. All animations are sequenced with
flags so that Godot's _process() loop cannot accidentally
override mid-sequence states (e.g. snapping the enemy back
to idle while an attack animation is playing).

----------------------------------------------------------------

AUDIO

The following sound effects are implemented and triggered
at the appropriate game moments:

  - Background music: loops continuously from game start.
  - Card hover sound: plays when the mouse enters a card.
  - Card click sound: plays when a card is clicked.
  - Card shuffle sound: plays on initial deal, end-turn
	reshuffle, and game-end card collection.
  - Attack sound: plays when the player attacks.
  - Hit sound: plays when the enemy is hit and when the
	player is hit during decoherence retaliation.
  - Decoherence sound: plays instantly on decoherence trigger.
  - Victory sound: plays when the player wins.
  - Game over sound: plays when the player loses.

----------------------------------------------------------------

WIN AND LOSE CONDITIONS

  WIN  — All three stats (Access, Quality, Equity) reach 100
		 after an End Turn collapse. The enemy plays its death
		 animation, the player celebrates, and a closing message
		 appears: "Crisis Contained. The Education Realm
		 stabilizes. The system holds, for now..."

  LOSE — Either pressure reaches 100 (overwhelmed by time),
		 or decoherence is triggered 5 times (the quantum
		 system fully destabilised). Both trigger a game over
		 with the appropriate message.

================================================================
DEVELOPMENT ASSISTANCE — WHAT WAS BUILT COLLABORATIVELY
================================================================

The following describes the systems and features that were
designed and implemented with the help of an AI assistant
(Claude) during the development of this project.

----------------------------------------------------------------

QUANTUM MECHANICS IMPLEMENTATION

The most significant collaborative contribution was translating
quantum mechanical concepts into concrete, playable game
systems. The idea of using superposition and decoherence as
gameplay mechanics came from the project's theme, but turning
those ideas into working GDScript logic required designing data
structures and interaction rules that genuinely reflected the
physics concepts rather than just borrowing their names.

The superposition system was built from scratch — each stat
needed to track not one value but a range (min and max bounds),
and every card play needed to widen that range in a way that
felt meaningful. The asymmetric split (60% low / 140% high)
was chosen so that the upper bound always grows faster than
the lower, creating genuine uncertainty about where the
collapse will land. The three-bar visual system (coloured main
bar, white upper bound, dark lower bound) was designed and
coded entirely at runtime in GDScript to represent that
superposition state clearly without cluttering the scene file.

The decoherence system involved designing a probability
mechanic that escalates with player greed. The doubling
formula (numerator starts at 1, doubles each safe card,
resets on trigger) was chosen specifically to mirror how
real decoherence becomes more likely as a quantum system
interacts more with its environment. Translating this into
an in-game risk/reward dilemma — do I play another card
and risk collapsing my progress, or do I end my turn now —
was a core part of the design work done collaboratively.



----------------------------------------------------------------

CARD SYSTEM POLISH

The card hover effect was implemented with a deliberate
design choice: scaling the Sprite2D child rather than the
root Node2D. This was necessary because scaling the root
would also scale the Area2D collision shape, causing
Godot to fire mouse_exited immediately after mouse_entered
as the shape boundary shifted under the cursor. By targeting
only the sprite, the collision area stays stable and the
hover effect works correctly. The TRANS_BACK easing was
chosen to give a springy, tactile overshoot feel.


A cards_enabled flag was added to the card manager to lock
the hand during animations, preventing the player from
clicking multiple cards before the first one finishes
its fly-to-deck sequence.

----------------------------------------------------------------

UI AND FEEDBACK SYSTEMS

The decoherence progress bar was given a ghost preview bar
that always shows where the bar will be after the next card
is played (the next doubling), so the player can see the
risk growing in real time. The same ghost preview pattern
was applied to the pressure bar, showing the cost of the
next End Turn before the player commits to it.

The A, Q, E stat labels were given a colour change system
that turns them yellow when their respective stat is fully
maxed — but only after End Turn resolves, not during card
play. This was a deliberate design choice to preserve the
superposition mystery: the player should not know for
certain whether a stat has been maxed until the wave
function collapses.
